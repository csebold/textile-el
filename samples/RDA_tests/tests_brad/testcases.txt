// Newly Digital -- Comprehensive regression test

// Article 1

The following is my entry for "Newly Digital":http://kalsey.com/2003/05/newly_digital/ -- a distributed writing experiment (thanks to "Adam Kalsey":http://kalsey.com/ for bringing the project together). ("Other entries on this theme.":/past/newly_digital.php#participants)

When I was a wee lad -- around 9 or so, I got my first game console. It was a "Pong" knockoff that only played Pong. I think we got it through mail order from Sears or some place. A year or two later my parents made the mistake of buying an Atari 2600 for the family. Things have never been the same since.

[!(>/past/images/atari_combat_cart.jpg(Atari 2600 Combat cartridge)!]Computers had been around for a while -- for business and government, that is. But consumer-grade computing (or home computing), well, that was starting to really take off in the 80's. I recall seeing "Wargames":http://us.imdb.com/Title?0086567 for the first time. David Lightman became my hero. _And_ he gets the girl! Yeah, right. The only part of the movie that's implausible. :)

Around '82 my parents bought a computer for their office work. It was an Olivetti word processor. I can't even find a picture of it online... it's quite obscure. At the time I didn't know what CPM was, but it wouldn't do me much good anyway. This thing was wired to run word processing software and nothing more. How disappointing. So I waited some more.

By '84 I was dying to get a computer. Of any kind. Desperation was setting in. I was looking high and low for something that would be affordable enough to persuade the folks. The "TRS-80 Color Computer":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=91 was one I looked at (we had "TRS-80s":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=18 in our school so I had a little exposure to "Trash" BASIC). The "TI-99 4A":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=236 was another. The "Coleco Adam":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=57 looked interesting -- a friend of mine got one. I even considered the "Commodore 64":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=98, although the "128":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=96 was going to be out soon and I just couldn't wait for it.

[!<)/past/images/apple_iic.gif(Apple IIc)!]The "Apple [_II_]c":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=69 was announced in April of '84 and thereafter I was dreaming at night about it. Portable and cool. Cool only like Apple can do. That was what I wanted. Every time I came in range of a computer store I would look for it. Any time I saw a magazine with a picture of it, I would glean all I could from it's pages.

The "IBM PCjr":http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&c=186 was what we settled on. I think perhaps because it was relatively cheap (compared to the Apple [_II_]c anyway) and came from a well-known and established company. It was family-friendly and expandable. It wasn't my first choice, but I was thankful. My parents probably had no idea what they were setting in motion and I'm sure they regretted it at times in the remaining years I was at home. I don't think I've had a proper night's sleep since.

[!>(/past/images/pcjr_mascots_small.gif(IBM PCjr mascots)!]The Jr. wasn't the most popular device in the world, but at the time it was really a neat computer. Ahead of its time in some respects. It had a wireless keyboard (infrared). It had a port for a light pen and joystick. The graphics were ahead of the basic CGA as it could do 16 colors instead of the basic 4. And the sound chip was more advanced, capable of 3 sound channels (["compare":http://www.oldskool.org/shrines/pcjr_tandy/#sound]).

My early experiences with my Jr. are vague, but I do recall getting severe hand cramps from all the typing. I learned to type on a little toy typewriter at age 10 or so. (Sadly, the thing was missing the semicolon key, so the right-most home row letter was "L".) The cramps didn't come from learning to type, but from long, extended periods of typing. I was typing in everything I could into the thing. Such a creative outlet.

h1. Learning to program

I had a book with some BASIC programs and a few "COMPUTE!":http://www.myoldcomputers.com/museum/mag/compute.htm magazines. I would type up those programs line by line. Not knowing or understanding what I was typing. I just typed. And typed. And then I would type that magical three-letter word: @RUN@.

[!<)/past/images/pcjr_cartridge_basic.jpg(IBM PCjr Cartridge BASIC)!]And then I would always see that error message. Hmm. Line 70? @LIST 70@ ... _compare character by character to line 70 from the magazine_. OH! I didn't put in that comma there. So I would retype line 70 and hit F2 (which was short for "RUN" in BASIC). Hmm. Error on line 80. _Sigh._

And that's how I learned BASIC. It was a slow, sometimes painful process. But very rewarding in the end. Eventually I got "Cartridge BASIC":http://mail.magnaspeed.net/~mbbrutman/PCjr/pcjr_pictures.html#Cartridges for the Jr. which let me save my programs (prior to that, all that hard work vanished once I turned the computer off. I probably typed in some of those programs 3 or 4 times each. And naturally only one could be in memory at a time.)

I started writing my own programs. One that would let me use the computer as an electronic typewriter (writing to our "Brother daisy-wheel printer":http://www.instruction-manuals.co.uk/category/printers/brotmods/hr20.htm in realtime). @LPRINT@ and @INKEY$@ and the like. Drawing programs ([@PSET@], @CIRCLE@, @LINE@, oh the memories), silly games and such.

Thanks to a friend, I also got an early introduction to database programming. I couldn't run dBASE III+ on my PCjr, but I could run dBASE II. Primitive, but it introduced me to storing data in rows and columns. Indexing and data analysis. And writing code without line numbers. Blew my mind.

It was a great time. Back when the "net" meant CompuServe and your email address was two numbers separated by a comma. And broadband was 1200 baud. So many new things to learn and do. I still enjoy that about computers. There's always something new to learn and do. You never get bored. From BASIC to dBASE (and Clipper!) to C and assembler to Pascal to C++ to Perl to Java. Lots of languages under the bridge.

And there's still more to learn and do.

<p>The following is my entry for <a href="http://kalsey.com/2003/05/newly_digital/">Newly Digital</a> -- a distributed writing experiment (thanks to <a href="http://kalsey.com/">Adam Kalsey</a> for bringing the project together). (<a href="/past/newly_digital.php#participants">Other entries on this theme.</a>)</p>

<p>When I was a wee lad -- around 9 or so, I got my first game console. It was a "Pong" knockoff that only played Pong. I think we got it through mail order from Sears or some place. A year or two later my parents made the mistake of buying an Atari 2600 for the family. Things have never been the same since.</p>

<p><img src="/past/images/atari_combat_cart.jpg" alt="Atari 2600 Combat cartridge" class="right" style="float:right;padding-left:1em" />Computers had been around for a while -- for business and government, that is. But consumer-grade computing (or home computing), well, that was starting to really take off in the 80's. I recall seeing <a href="http://us.imdb.com/Title?0086567">Wargames</a> for the first time. David Lightman became my hero. <em>And</em> he gets the girl! Yeah, right. The only part of the movie that's implausible. :)</p>

<p>Around '82 my parents bought a computer for their office work. It was an Olivetti word processor. I can't even find a picture of it online... it's quite obscure. At the time I didn't know what <span class="caps">CPM </span>was, but it wouldn't do me much good anyway. This thing was wired to run word processing software and nothing more. How disappointing. So I waited some more.</p>

<p>By '84 I was dying to get a computer. Of any kind. Desperation was setting in. I was looking high and low for something that would be affordable enough to persuade the folks. The <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=91"><span class="caps">TRS</span>-80 Color Computer</a> was one I looked at (we had <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=18"><span class="caps">TRS</span>-80s</a> in our school so I had a little exposure to "Trash" BASIC). The <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=236">TI-99 4A</a> was another. The <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=57">Coleco Adam</a> looked interesting -- a friend of mine got one. I even considered the <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=98">Commodore 64</a>, although the <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=96">128</a> was going to be out soon and I just couldn't wait for it.</p>

<p><img src="/past/images/apple_iic.gif" alt="Apple IIc" class="left" style="float:left;padding-right:1em" />The <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=69">Apple <em>II</em>c</a> was announced in April of '84 and thereafter I was dreaming at night about it. Portable and cool. Cool only like Apple can do. That was what I wanted. Every time I came in range of a computer store I would look for it. Any time I saw a magazine with a picture of it, I would glean all I could from it's pages.</p>

<p>The <a href="http://www.old-computers.com/museum/computer.asp?st=1&amp;c=186"><span class="caps">IBM</span> PCjr</a> was what we settled on. I think perhaps because it was relatively cheap (compared to the Apple <em>II</em>c anyway) and came from a well-known and established company. It was family-friendly and expandable. It wasn't my first choice, but I was thankful. My parents probably had no idea what they were setting in motion and I'm sure they regretted it at times in the remaining years I was at home. I don't think I've had a proper night's sleep since.</p>

<p><img src="/past/images/pcjr_mascots_small.gif" alt="IBM PCjr mascots" class="right" style="float:right;padding-left:1em" />The Jr. wasn't the most popular device in the world, but at the time it was really a neat computer. Ahead of its time in some respects. It had a wireless keyboard (infrared). It had a port for a light pen and joystick. The graphics were ahead of the basic <span class="caps">CGA </span>as it could do 16 colors instead of the basic 4. And the sound chip was more advanced, capable of 3 sound channels (<a href="http://www.oldskool.org/shrines/pcjr_tandy/#sound">compare</a>).</p>

<p>My early experiences with my Jr. are vague, but I do recall getting severe hand cramps from all the typing. I learned to type on a little toy typewriter at age 10 or so. (Sadly, the thing was missing the semicolon key, so the right-most home row letter was "L".) The cramps didn't come from learning to type, but from long, extended periods of typing. I was typing in everything I could into the thing. Such a creative outlet.</p>

<h1>Learning to program</h1>

<p>I had a book with some <span class="caps">BASIC </span>programs and a few <a href="http://www.myoldcomputers.com/museum/mag/compute.htm"><span class="caps">COMPUTE</span>!</a> magazines. I would type up those programs line by line. Not knowing or understanding what I was typing. I just typed. And typed. And then I would type that magical three-letter word: <code>RUN</code>.</p>

<p><img src="/past/images/pcjr_cartridge_basic.jpg" alt="IBM PCjr Cartridge BASIC" class="left" style="float:left;padding-right:1em" />And then I would always see that error message. Hmm. Line 70? <code>LIST 70</code> ... <em>compare character by character to line 70 from the magazine</em>. OH! I didn't put in that comma there. So I would retype line 70 and hit F2 (which was short for "RUN" in <span class="caps">BASIC</span>). Hmm. Error on line 80. <em>Sigh.</em></p>

<p>And that's how I learned <span class="caps">BASIC.</span> It was a slow, sometimes painful process. But very rewarding in the end. Eventually I got <a href="http://mail.magnaspeed.net/~mbbrutman/PCjr/pcjr_pictures.html#Cartridges">Cartridge <span class="caps">BASIC</span></a> for the Jr. which let me save my programs (prior to that, all that hard work vanished once I turned the computer off. I probably typed in some of those programs 3 or 4 times each. And naturally only one could be in memory at a time.)</p>

<p>I started writing my own programs. One that would let me use the computer as an electronic typewriter (writing to our <a href="http://www.instruction-manuals.co.uk/category/printers/brotmods/hr20.htm">Brother daisy-wheel printer</a> in realtime). <code>LPRINT</code> and <code>INKEY$</code> and the like. Drawing programs (<code>PSET</code>, <code>CIRCLE</code>, <code>LINE</code>, oh the memories), silly games and such.</p>

<p>Thanks to a friend, I also got an early introduction to database programming. I couldn't run dBASE <span class="caps">III</span>+ on my PCjr, but I could run dBASE <span class="caps">II.</span> Primitive, but it introduced me to storing data in rows and columns. Indexing and data analysis. And writing code without line numbers. Blew my mind.</p>

<p>It was a great time. Back when the "net" meant CompuServe and your email address was two numbers separated by a comma. And broadband was 1200 baud. So many new things to learn and do. I still enjoy that about computers. There's always something new to learn and do. You never get bored. From <span class="caps">BASIC </span>to dBASE (and Clipper!) to C and assembler to Pascal to C++ to Perl to Java. Lots of languages under the bridge.</p>

<p>And there's still more to learn and do.</p>
